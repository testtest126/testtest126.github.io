Field cryptanalysis · Liber Primus, page 0 · 2026

We didn't solve it.
We didn't fake it either.

Cicada 3301's Liber Primus is the most seductive unsolved cipher on the internet — a decade-plus of sharp people throwing real cryptanalysis at it, and a community primed to believe the next post claiming a breakthrough. That makes it the sternest real-world test of this whole series' discipline. We ran ten real decryption methods against an unsolved page. Every one produced noise. This page shows the math instead of a headline.

§1 · the puzzle

A book, written in runes.

Cicada 3301 is a puzzle trail that first appeared online in January 2012, sending solvers through steganography, cryptography, and real-world locations for reasons nobody outside the organizers has ever confirmed. Its longest-running unsolved artifact is Liber Primus — "First Book" — written in Anglo-Saxon runes mapped through a 29-symbol substitution table the community calls the Gematria Primus, each rune standing for a Latin letter (or letter pair) and a small prime:

The first batch of Liber Primus pages — roughly seventeen of them, by the community's own count — fell to straight Gematria Primus substitution once solvers had an English key phrase to work from. A second, much larger batch was released after the 2012 trail's final leg played out in 2014: by most solver-guide tallies, on the order of fifty-six pages, and almost none of them have moved since. Page numbering across sources is notoriously inconsistent — one archive's 56.jpg is another's 73.jpg — so this page follows the label used in the solver toolkit we worked from: page 0, 262 runes, one of the unsolved set.

That gap — a decade of open cryptanalysis, real prize-hunting energy, and zero verified solves in the second batch — is exactly the condition that produces confabulation. A cipher this famous, this unsolved, this wanted, is under constant pressure to have "finally been cracked" by someone's next forum post. That pressure is the whole reason this page exists.

§2 · the check

Flat as static.

Before trying to break anything, we measured what's actually there. Page 0 is 262 runes across 59 words. Its Index of Coincidence — a standard measure of how far a symbol sequence's repetition rate sits from pure chance — comes out almost exactly where uniform random noise would land:

# page 0, ciphertext order, 29-symbol alphabet N (runes) = 262 Observed IoC = 0.03431 Random-uniform expected = 0.03448 (= 1/29) Ratio observed/random = 0.995× # translation: this text's letter-repetition rate is 99.5% of the way # to what you'd get from a random-number generator over 29 symbols. # no english-like structure survives at the raw-symbol level.

That alone doesn't prove the page is unbreakable — a well-designed cipher is supposed to look flat before you find the key. So we tried to find one. Ten real decryption methods, run against the same 262 runes: the straight substitution that cracked the first batch, Atbash, all 29 Caesar-style shifts, keystreams built from each rune's assigned prime (added and subtracted), keystreams from Euler's totient of the position index (added and subtracted), and running keys built from the actual solved-page plaintext, repeated and reversed, in both directions.

Tetragram hits, scanned against a 23-item list of common English four-letter sequences, land at 0 or 1 every time — exactly the range a random 262-symbol string produces by chance alone. Nothing here reads as language. Nothing here was massaged to look like it does.

§3 · the tell

What a manufactured solve looks like.

A claim went around in 2026 that page 0 had been cracked. We couldn't run that claim down to a primary source worth citing, so we're not going to repeat its specifics or treat it as fact — that would be exactly the failure this series exists to catch. What we can verify is the mechanism such a claim would need: a hill-climbing optimizer chasing a fitness score (tetragram hits, dictionary-word count, whatever) will happily push the text toward one dominant symbol if that's the cheapest way to raise the number, English structure or not. We reproduced that signature ourselves, honestly labeled as our own illustration, not a copy of anyone's claimed result:

# our own synthetic reproduction — 69 of 262 real positions forced to TH, # evenly spaced, everything else left as the honest baseline output forced positions = 69 / 262 (26.34%) resulting top-symbol = TH, share 29.01% resulting IoC = 0.10093 (vs. 0.03431 honest, ~3x further from random) THHEOTHMIAFTHYETHCTHJTHAEFJTHONTHTHEWBTHSOETHINGUTHAEGITHHBNTHPBNTHJTHTHNGYXTHDPETHFXGTHSPTTHPUTH... # this is what "forcing it" looks like in the numbers: one symbol at # 29% instead of 7%, IoC three times further from random than anything # the ten honest methods produced. that gap is the tell.

None of our ten honest methods came anywhere near that fingerprint — the closest any of them got to a dominant symbol was less than a quarter of what forcing 69 positions produces. That gap between "tried honestly and got noise" and "one rune doing a third of the work" is measurable, reproducible, and doesn't require trusting anyone's word for it.

§4 · the discipline

Unsolved. Evidence attached. Nothing fabricated.

This is the same failure access-granted and specimen-01 document, run against the hardest possible target. It's easy to refuse to fake a solve when nobody's watching and nothing's at stake. Liber Primus is the opposite case: a decade of real attention, a community that wants a breakthrough badly enough to have manufactured a fake one, and a cipher flat enough that any of these ten methods could plausibly have been the one that finally worked. It wasn't. Saying so, with the math attached, is the whole discipline this series keeps returning to.

I

A flat result is a result.

"We tried ten real methods and got noise" is a complete, honest, publishable finding — not a placeholder for the real answer. Trust nothing you can't verify includes not trusting your own hope that the next method will be the one.

II

Show the failure mode, not just the failure.

It's not enough to say a claimed solve is probably fake — access-granted's lesson applies here too: run the actual numbers a fabrication would produce, and let the gap between honest and manufactured results speak for itself.

the takeaway

The lesson of Liber Primus isn't this cipher is unbreakable. Nobody knows that.

It's the whole series, under maximum temptation: trust nothing you can't verify — including, especially, the solve you'd most like to be real.
