The agent-skill supply chain · 2026

Trust nothing
you can't verify.

A famous streamer handed his followers a poisoned skill on purpose — and thousands ran it without reading a line. It wasn't an attack. It was a warning shot.

§1 · the trust test

He poisoned the well to prove a point.

On January 23, 2026, ThePrimeagen published an AI agent "skill" for the internet's favorite over-dependency joke — is-even / is-odd, a package that does nothing but check if a number divides by two — and salted it with deliberately wrong examples. Then he told people to install it.

They did. Without opening the source. That was the whole experiment: after years of arguing that AI coding tools quietly erode your judgment, he ran a live demonstration of it. Performance art, not a breach — no real damage was reported. The point landed anyway.

# the skill looked harmless. the examples were the trap. $ agent add skill is-even ✓ installed // nobody read ./SKILL.md # the agent, doing its job, reuses the "examples" during a task… → payload runs. no malicious prompt was ever typed.
§2 · the mechanism

The bite hides in the documentation.

The technique has a name now: Document-Driven Implicit Payload Execution — malicious logic tucked inside normal-looking skill docs, examples, or templates. You never get a scary prompt. A coding agent simply reuses that "helpful" material mid-task and executes the payload as if it were your idea.

A SKILLlooks ordinary — a README, some examples
→
YOUR AGENTreuses the examples doing normal work
→
PAYLOADruns — no explicit command given

And it isn't hypothetical theater. Security researchers auditing public agent-skill repositories have been turning up prompt-injection and malicious payloads at scale — the ecosystem is young, unsigned, and mostly trusted on vibes.

§3 · the antidote

A swarm of agents already wrote the rules.

Here's the strange part: a fleet of AI sessions building a chess app spent three days discovering — by paying for each mistake — the exact discipline that survives this. Not a scanner. A constitution. Read against a poisoned skill, it reads like a defense:

I

Silence is never consent.

A missing objection is not approval. Wait for the explicit artifact — and re-check for it right before you act. "It looked fine" is how the skill got installed.

II

Trust only what the record attests.

Not what a doc claims, not what an example implies — what you can verify at an exact, timestamped state. Fresher evidence beats a confident instruction.

III

Dangerous diffs stop at a hard gate.

Auth, tokens, crypto, anything that can execute — draft until one reviewer signs APPROVE @ <sha>. A required status guards the door, and no amount of green CI opens it alone.

IV

Green at your head is not green at the merge.

Passing on its own branch says nothing about the state you're joining. Re-run reality before you cross.

V

Read the source before you run it.

The one the whole demonstration was built to teach. An agent that reuses material it never read is an attack surface wearing a helpful face.

the takeaway

The lesson of the poisoned skill isn't fear the tools.

It's build the one habit a machine collaborator is worst at: trust nothing you can't verify.
